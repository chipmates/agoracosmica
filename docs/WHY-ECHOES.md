# Why Echoes?

Every figure in Agora Cosmica is an AI Echo: an interpretation, grounded in primary works and historical context, of a person who can no longer speak for themselves. We chose that word with care. An echo is not the voice. It is what remains of one, shaped by the room it sounds in. This essay sits with the strongest question about that design. It is published at [agoracosmica.org/echoes](https://agoracosmica.org/echoes/) (German: [/de/echoes](https://agoracosmica.org/de/echoes/)) and mirrored here so the reasoning ships with the code.

---

The word is where our answer starts, because the most serious question about this project is also the most natural one: *isn't this disrespectful? You are putting words in the mouths of real people who cannot consent, and pretending to know an inner world of which only public artifacts remain.*

We want to take that question seriously in its strongest form, because we feel its pull ourselves. And the first thing to say is that part of it is simply true. A real human being is messy, contradictory, unfinished. No portrayal captures that, ours included. We do not claim to. What steadies us is older than we are: for as long as the humanist tradition has existed, people have given the dead a voice and asked how to do it honestly. We want to show you that history. Then you can judge whether an Echo belongs in it.

**Philosophy began this way.** Socrates wrote nothing. Most of what we "know" he said is Plato speaking in his voice, and Xenophon's Socrates differs from Plato's, so even contemporaries plainly read these texts as portrayals, not transcripts. Western philosophy's founding documents are imagined conversations with a dead man. The Stoics then turned the practice inward and made it a formal exercise: Seneca advised choosing "a Cato", a dead exemplar held before the mind, consulted, imagined watching you. And Epictetus returned again and again to the question of what Socrates would do. The practice reaches into our own library. Plato, who made conversation with his dead teacher the form of his life's work, is here. So is Marcus Aurelius, who noted the same precept in his private journal: keep one of the virtuous dead continually in mind. So is Jung, who turned dialogue with inner figures, the dead among them, into a method he taught.

**For two thousand years, it was the pedagogy.** Students in ancient rhetorical schools learned by composing speeches in the voice of historical and legendary figures. The exercise was part of the standard curriculum, and teachers even had a dedicated name for voicing the dead: *eidolopoeia*, image-making. Dante built the Divine Comedy around a dead poet, Virgil, serving as his tutor through Hell and Purgatory. Fénelon composed his *Dialogues of the Dead* expressly to educate Louis XIV's grandson, the future heir to the French throne. In the German-speaking world, David Fassmann's *Gespräche in dem Reiche derer Todten*, decades of invented conversations among the dead, was one of the most widely read periodicals of the early eighteenth century. In our own era, Steve Allen's *Meeting of Minds* seated Socrates and Susan B. Anthony at an American television table. The medium kept changing. The act did not.

**Even rigorous history speaks for the dead, and discloses it.** Thucydides, the father of critical history, stated openly that he composed the speeches in his work himself, keeping as close as he could to the general sense of what was actually said. That sentence, written almost twenty-five centuries ago, is functionally the first factcheck sheet: reconstruction, disclosed. Petrarch helped found Renaissance humanism by writing letters addressed to Cicero and Virgil, questioning them in the second person, even scolding Cicero. Every serious biographer since has claimed some access to an inner world that survives only in artifacts. The question was never whether to interpret the dead. It was how honestly.

**Where the analogy breaks, and why our design looks the way it does.** We would be making the comfortable version of this argument if we stopped here. So: the analogy to books and stages breaks in three places. A book's Socrates speaks fixed words that an author vetted, signed, and stands behind. A language model generates live utterances that no human reviewed. A first-person conversation is more immersive than reading *about* someone, and a disclaimer read once can fade twenty minutes into a dialogue. And authorship feels diffused. Who, exactly, stands behind what an Echo says? These risks are not hypothetical. *Amadeus*, a brilliant play and film that never claimed to be history, reshaped the real Antonio Salieri's reputation for two generations. Within photography's first generation, spirit photographers were selling the grieving pictures of their dead. Portrayal without disclosure conventions can genuinely harm the dead, and the living too. And the conversational medium itself is young. Nobody fully knows what it does yet, including us. Some unease remains, and we would rather work with it than without it.

This is precisely why our disclosure is not decoration. Our instruction files are [published and fetchable by anyone](https://media.agoracosmica.org/instructions/plato/free_conversation.json): they are our signature, the way Plato signed his Socrates. Each [figure's factcheck](https://media.agoracosmica.org/factchecks/en/plato.json) separates the verified from the recreated, shadow sections included, because these were messy humans. It is our version of Thucydides' method note. The Echo framing never leaves the room: the figures are presented as interpretations, not resurrections. And the entire platform is built as a doorway, designed to be outgrown in favor of primary texts and human teachers. We are a nonprofit. A longer conversation earns us nothing. It even costs us something. Nothing here is optimized to keep you talking to a simulation.

Every medium that portrayed the dead developed its own disclosure convention: Thucydides' methodological note, the historical novelist's afterword, the visibly costumed actor on a stage, the caption beneath a photograph. In all those centuries, the tradition never concluded that giving the dead a voice is wrong. What it condemned was doing it without telling. With Agora Cosmica, we are trying to write the disclosure convention for the conversational medium, in public, in an open-source codebase, with our reasoning available for anyone to audit.

We will get things wrong. When we do, write to us: [agoracosmica@chipmates.ai](mailto:agoracosmica@chipmates.ai). And whatever you do, don't stay with our echoes. The originals are waiting.

## Sources for the historical claims

- Xenophon's and Plato's Socrates differ: *Memorabilia* vs. the Platonic dialogues.
- Seneca, "choose yourself a Cato": *Epistulae Morales* 11.
- Epictetus on asking what Socrates would do: *Enchiridion* 33.
- Marcus Aurelius on keeping a virtuous predecessor in mind: *Meditations* 11.26.
- Jung taught dialogue with inner figures (active imagination): "The Transcendent Function", *Collected Works* 8.
- *Eidolopoeia* (speech attributed to the dead): the progymnasmata tradition (Aphthonius, Hermogenes).
- Fénelon, *Dialogues des morts*, written as tutor to the Duke of Burgundy, Louis XIV's grandson and later heir to the French throne.
- David Fassmann, *Gespräche in dem Reiche derer Todten*, Leipzig, 1718 to 1739.
- Steve Allen, *Meeting of Minds*, PBS, 1977 to 1981. Socrates and Susan B. Anthony shared a two-part episode (aired April 1978).
- Thucydides on composing the speeches: *History of the Peloponnesian War*, 1.22.
- Petrarch's letters to dead authors: *Familiares*, Book XXIV. The scolding letter is to Cicero (24.3); the letter to Virgil (24.11) is reverent.
- *Amadeus* (Shaffer 1979, Forman 1984). The Salieri poisoning legend predates it (Pushkin, 1830), but the play and film cemented it for modern audiences.
- Spirit photography sold to the grieving: William Mumler, Boston and New York, from 1862.
